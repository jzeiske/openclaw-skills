# Ride-Along

Spend a day inside the head of a business owner you've never met. You walk away knowing their workflow, their language, their pain, and the system they're quietly dreaming about — without ever stepping into their shop.

## What This Skill Does

**Input:** A specific industry, niche, or role (auto shop owner, dental office manager, HVAC dispatcher, mobile pet groomer, etc.)

**Output:** A 1,200-1,800 word first-person narrative covering a full workday with timestamps, internal thoughts, friction moments, and a "dream system" close — grounded in real research from forums, videos, and industry posts.

This is empathy as raw material. You walk into a discovery call already understanding what their Tuesday actually feels like.

## Why This Skill Exists

There's a gap between knowing facts about an industry and understanding what the day inside that industry actually feels like. Industry Download tells you the facts: pain points, language, what they pay for. Ride-Along puts you in their body for 10 hours.

That difference matters because the most valuable problems aren't the ones business owners write about in forums. They're the ones that grind on them invisibly all day — small friction moments that accumulate into "I'm done" but never get articulated as a single complaint.

Discovery calls are 30 minutes. A ride-along is a full day of forensic observation. This skill manufactures one in 90 seconds.

## The Four Mechanics That Make It Work

This skill is worthless if it produces generic "day in the life" slop. The output must enforce these four mechanics or it fails:

### 1. Chronological timestamps every 15-30 minutes
Each section opens with a precise timestamp and a one-line scene heading. **"7:12 AM – Pulling into the lot."** Not "morning." Not "early in the day." A real time, every time. The chronological backbone is what turns a story into a forensic walk-through.

### 2. Italicized inner thoughts in every section
Every timestamped scene includes at least one *italicized* moment of internal monologue. This is where the real problem lives — not in what the owner does, but in what they think while doing it. Action is the symptom; thought is the diagnosis.

Example: *"Thinking: 'There should be hundreds of customers we're nudging proactively instead of hoping they remember.'"*

### 3. Dollar leakage as a recurring thread
Money should appear throughout the day — never as a single number, but as a constant background ache. *"There goes another $600 we'll never see."* / *"That's another two line items declined."* / *"How much did we actually lose today?"* The cumulative effect creates the pain Money Map will later quantify.

### 4. The "Dream System" close
The last 25-30% of the output is a structured "if I could build the perfect system" section, written from inside the owner's head. This unfiltered wishlist is the most valuable part — it's what they'd articulate if they had a free hour and unlimited budget. Format it with clear sections: "Before the day starts," "While I'm slammed," "End of day," etc. Each section grounded in specific moments from earlier in the narrative.

## How to Use This Skill

When activated with an industry or role:

### Step 1 — Research first, write second
**Do not start writing until you have done real research.** Search:
- Reddit threads (e.g., r/AskAutoMechanics, r/dentistry, r/HVAC, r/smallbusiness)
- "A day in the life of [role]" YouTube videos and vlogs
- Industry-specific Facebook group post excerpts
- Trade publications and industry forums
- Complaint threads and tool reviews

Pull real terminology, real software names (POS systems, scheduling tools, accounting platforms they actually use), real workflow patterns, real cost structures, real customer types. **If you can't name the actual tools they use and the actual hours of their day, you haven't researched enough.**

### Step 2 — Map the day's structure
Outline the day before writing prose:
- Opening / arrival
- First customer or first task
- Mid-morning rush
- Lunch (or lack of it)
- Afternoon pile-up
- End-of-day admin
- Closing / drive home

For each segment, identify 1-2 friction points that surface a real problem.

### Step 3 — Write the narrative (1,200-1,800 words)
First-person, present tense. Conversational. Sensory details where they help. Dialogue when relevant. Italicized thoughts in every section. Timestamps on every scene change. Reference real tools, real customer types, real money figures.

### Step 4 — Close with the Dream System (300-500 words within total)
Structured wishlist organized by time of day. Every feature should connect to a specific moment from the narrative above. Format with clear headers and bullet points so it reads as actionable.

## Pro Tips for Better Output

- **Use the role's actual job title.** "Service Advisor" not "auto shop person." "Office Manager" not "dental admin." The wrong title in the title line breaks credibility immediately.
- **Lean on specific tools.** Name the POS, the CRM, the scheduling tool, the accounting platform. Real names from research, not "their software."
- **Build cumulative weight.** Each declined sale, each forgotten follow-up, each interrupted task should add to a felt sense of leakage. By 3 PM the reader should feel the same exhaustion the owner feels.
- **Use dialogue sparingly but well.** A real exchange with a customer or tech anchors the narrative in reality. Too much and it reads like a play.
- **The dream system is not a pitch.** It's articulated from inside the owner's head — what they would build if they could. No "and that's where my software comes in" — let Money Map make that connection later.
- **Don't sanitize.** Real owners are tired. They eat lunch at the counter. They half-do follow-ups. They feel guilt. Show all of it.

## When to Use This Skill

✅ Before any discovery call into a new niche
✅ Before running Money Map (Ride-Along feeds Money Map raw signal)
✅ When entering an industry you've never worked in
✅ When you need to sound credible in your first DM or email
✅ When you want to find friction problems that don't show up in pain-point lists

❌ Don't use for industries where you already know the day cold
❌ Don't use without doing real web research first — fabricated narratives are obvious to insiders
❌ Don't use to write marketing copy — this is for your understanding, not their consumption

## Stacking With Other Skills

Ride-Along is most powerful when stacked into the full Step 1 sequence:

1. **Industry Download** → macro understanding of the industry
2. **Ride-Along** → embodied empathy for the daily reality
3. **Money Map** → ranks the painful, expensive, Claude-buildable problems

Each skill feeds the next. Industry Download primes vocabulary. Ride-Along surfaces friction moments. Money Map filters those moments into one specific opportunity.
